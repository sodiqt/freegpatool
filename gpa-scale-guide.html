There is no single global GPA scale. Here's how the major systems work, what counts as a top grade in each, and rough (not official) equivalencies between them.

United States: 4.0 scale

Letter grades A through F map to points from 4.0 down to 0.0, often with plus/minus increments (A- = 3.7, B+ = 3.3, and so on). Some competitive high schools add a weighted 5.0 scale for Honors, AP, or IB courses.

UK universities don't typically use a decimal GPA at all — the classification band itself is what appears on transcripts and diplomas.

Canada: 4.0 / 4.33 scale

Most Canadian universities use a 4.0 or 4.33 scale (the extra 0.33 allows an A+ to outrank a plain A), though some provinces, including parts of Ontario, report percentages instead.

Nigerian universities calculate CGPA cumulatively across every session using the same quality-points-over-credit-units formula as the US system, just scaled to 5.0 instead of 4.0. See the full Nigeria GPA guide for a worked example.

FAQ

Why do grading scales differ by country?

Each country's education system developed its grading approach independently, often shaped by distinct historical university traditions — there's no single global standard.

How do I convert my GPA for a foreign university application?

Use the destination university's official conversion table when one is published, since institutions typically provide their own equivalency guidance for applicants coming from a different grading system.
