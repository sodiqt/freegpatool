GPA vs CGPA: What's the Real Difference?

They get used interchangeably all the time, but GPA and CGPA answer two different questions — one about a single term, one about your entire academic record.

The core definitions

GPA

Grade Point Average for a single term — usually one semester or quarter. Resets in scope every term, though the transcript keeps every term's history.

CGPA

Cumulative Grade Point Average across every graded term completed so far. Recalculated each time a new term's grades are added.

Mechanically, both use the identical formula — total quality points divided by total credit hours. The only difference is the window of courses included: one term for GPA, every term for CGPA.

That's the practical value of CGPA: a rough term shows up, but it's diluted by every other term rather than standing alone. Track both figures with the GPA calculator as each term ends.

Which one actually matters?

  • Scholarships: Most renewal requirements are based on cumulative CGPA, not the most recent semester alone.
  • Grad school and professional programs: Admissions committees almost always request CGPA, sometimes alongside your GPA trend over the final two years to check for improvement.
  • Academic probation: Many institutions trigger probation based on a CGPA threshold, so a single bad semester GPA might not trigger it if your CGPA stays above the line.
  • Dean's List or term honors: These are usually based on that specific semester's GPA, not the cumulative figure.

Where people mix them up

The most common error is assuming CGPA is a simple average of each term's GPA — for example, averaging 3.20, 2.80, and 3.70 to get 3.23. That's close but not exact, because it ignores that each semester carried a different number of credits. The correct method always works from total quality points and total credits across all terms, not an average of averages.

FAQ

Is CGPA the average of all my GPAs?

Not exactly — it's total quality points across all terms divided by total credits attempted, which only matches a simple average if every term had identical credit loads.

Which one do employers and grad schools look at?

Most graduate programs and employers request cumulative GPA, though a clear upward trend in your semester GPA is worth highlighting separately in an application.

Can one bad semester ruin my CGPA?

It has less impact on CGPA than it would on a standalone GPA, since it's blended with every other term — though the effect is larger earlier in a program, when fewer total credits exist to dilute it.
